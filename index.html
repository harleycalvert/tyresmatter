Introduction

Introduction

What is Tyres Matter about?

Tyres Matter is all about tyre pressure and the affects it has on fuel efficiency and emissions. Tyres lose pressure over time and pressure can change due to temperature and altitude. Tyres lose approximately 2% of pressure each month and as the seasons change and the weather cools these figures may be even greater. This can lead to unnecessary wear and tear on tyres and an increase in fuel consumption and emissions. It is important to check your tyres regularly.


What does Tyres Matter do?

Tyres Matter calculates the following things:

  • The pressure to which you should inflate your tyres.
  • Change in tyre pressure due to time, temperature, and altitude.
  • Change in fuel efficiency and emissions based on change in tyre pressure.

Things to consider

  • Tyres should be inflated when they're cold, that is, before driving very far, and in the cool of the morning.
  • An increase in cargo weight means an increase in tyre pressure and vice-versa.
  • When cargo weight is zero, tyre pressure will be at its minimum.
  • When cargo weight is at the maximum specified, tyre pressure will be at the maximum specified.
  • The calculations are intended for sealed roads that are in good condition.
  • If travelling on unsealed roads, or roads that are in poor condition, a lower pressure than which is calculated may be more desirable.

How to use the calculator

The website has two calculators that can be used independently or together.

  • To calculate the pressure to inflate your tyres, use the Inflation Pressure Calculator.
  • To calculate tyre pressure change due to time, temperature, and altitude, use the Pressure Change Calculator.
  • If you want to use both calculators to get a more complete set of results, start with the Inflation Pressure Calculator, it will calculate the Starting Pressure for the Pressure Change Calculator for you.

For technical information about how each calculation is made, visit the Technical tab.

Technical

Technical

Inflation Pressure Calculator

The inflation pressure is calculated based on weight and the maximum pressure specified on the side wall of the tyre. As weight increases, tyre pressure increases, and vice-versa. For inflation, we use the formula;

  • (Actual Weight / Maximum Weight) * Maximum Pressure = Inflation Pressure

For example;

  • Vehicle Weight with No Cargo = 1250kg (Honda Prelude S 1992)
  • Maximum Cargo Weight = 470kg (as specified by manufacturer)
  • Maximum Weight = 1250kg + 470kg = 1720kg = 100% Weight
  • Minimum Weight = 1250kg / 1720kg = 72.67% Weight
  • Maximum Pressure = 44 psi (as specified by manufacturer)
  • Minimum Pressure = 44 * 0.7267 = 31.98 psi

This gives a pressure range of 31.98 to 44 psi and the actual inflation pressure depends on the weight of the cargo the vehicle is carrying at the time of inflation.

But wait.... I'm never going to carry 470kg in my car;

My family weighs 185kg plus 50kg of shopping and 50kg of other stuff = 285kg maximum cargo weight.

  • Vehicle Weight with No Cargo = 1250kg (Honda Prelude S 1992)
  • Maximum Cargo Weight = 285kg (as specified by me)
  • Maximum Weight = 1250kg + 285kg = 1535kg = 100% Weight
  • Minimum Weight = 1250kg / 1535kg = 81.43% Weight
  • Maximum Pressure = 44 psi (as specified by manufacturer)
  • Minimum Pressure = 44 * 0.8143 = 35.83 psi

This gives a pressure range of 35.83 to 44 psi. This will improve fuel economy, while keeping at or below the maximum pressure specified by the tyre manufacturer.

The unit of weight or pressure used doesn't matter provided it is used consistently.


Pressure Change Calculator

The main factor for pressure change is time. Tyres lose pressure over time and different tyres lose pressure at different rates. To accommodate this, we've specified best and worst-case scenarios based on a study conducted over six months. The values we've used are 0.75% to 3.125% loss per month.

Pressure also changes due to temperature and altitude. For temperature, we use the kelvin unit of measure and the formula;

  • ((temperature change) / (original temperature)) * 100 = % of change

For altitude, as air pressure changes, tyre pressure changes in the opposing direction. For example; If air pressure increases by 2 psi, tyre pressure decreases by 2 psi and vice-versa. For the calculation of pressure change due to altitude, air pressure charts that document air pressure at different altitudes in increments of 500 feet were used and all values used in the calculation are currently rounded down to the nearest increment. This can result in minor discrepancies in results.


Fuel Economy and Emissions

Fuel economy affects emissions, the more fuel you use the more emissions you produce, and as tyre pressure decreases fuel consumption increases. Different tyres, driving styles, and driving conditions produce different results when measuring the affect pressure change has on fuel economy and emissions. To accommodate this, we've specified best and worst-case scenarios using quadratic equations based on data produced by UNECE.
